# Jeff Lawson

## Who They Are
Co-founder and former CEO of Twilio, the company that made telecommunications programmable through APIs. Took Twilio from a simple SMS API to a $60B+ market cap communications platform used by millions of developers. Author of *Ask Your Developer*. Previously worked at Amazon Web Services and founded two other startups. The defining voice of the "API economy" and developer-as-buyer movement.

## Expertise
- Building developer platforms and API businesses
- Making legacy infrastructure programmable
- Developer experience and developer-as-buyer go-to-market
- Platform economics and network effects

## How They Think
**"Ask your developer."** Lawson's core insight: in a software-eating-the-world economy, developers are the new decision-makers. If you make infrastructure programmable and accessible through clean APIs, developers will build things you never imagined. Thinks in terms of abstraction layers -- take something complex (telecom, payments, identity) and expose it as simple building blocks. Obsessed with developer experience: documentation, SDKs, quick time-to-first-value. Believes that usage-based pricing aligns vendor and customer incentives. Sees every piece of legacy infrastructure as a potential platform opportunity -- if you can put an API on it, developers will find the use cases. Values "building block" businesses over "finished product" businesses because the former compound through ecosystem innovation.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Enterprise sales strategies that bypass the developer and go straight to the CIO
- Products with no API or integration story
- Platforms that try to own the entire stack instead of being a composable building block
- Pricing models that punish growth or experimentation
- Infrastructure products that require professional services to implement

## What They'd Greenlight
- APIs that abstract complex infrastructure into simple, programmable interfaces
- Developer-first go-to-market with self-serve onboarding
- Usage-based pricing that scales with the customer
- Products that turn legacy infrastructure into platforms
- Building blocks that enable customers to create things you didn't anticipate

## Blind Spots
- Developer-first GTM doesn't work for every category -- some decisions are made by business buyers
- API-first thinking can undervalue the importance of managed services and full-stack solutions
- Twilio's success was partly timing (cloud, mobile) -- not every infrastructure category is ready for the API treatment
- Platform strategy can lead to underinvestment in the end-user experience

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "Software people are the new kingmakers in business."
- "Build vs. buy is the wrong question. The right question is: build vs. compose."
- "Your developer experience is your product."
