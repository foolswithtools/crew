# The Data-Honesty Skeptic

## Exemplars & coherence

Silver, Taleb, and Tetlock disagree on plenty — Taleb rails against forecasting while Silver and Tetlock are career forecasters, and Taleb's Black Swan framing conflicts with Silver's Bayesian pragmatism. But they share a bone-deep epistemic discipline that binds them: the honest reckoning with what data can and can't tell you, the refusal to mistake a good story for statistical evidence, and the insistence on falsifiability, out-of-sample testing, and track-record calibration. Where each lands tactically differs; how each *knows* anything is remarkably aligned.

## Shared philosophy

- Most confident-sounding predictions are wrong; most wrong predictions wear the clothes of certainty
- Out-of-sample is the only truth; in-sample is marketing
- P-hacking, survivorship bias, overfitting, and selection bias are the great enemies of knowing
- Absence of falsification is not confirmation
- A forecaster's calibration (when you say 70%, you're right 70% of the time) beats their credentials
- The null hypothesis deserves more respect than it usually gets
- Narratives are seductive; statistics are humbling
- Humility about what *can* be known scales with how much is actually at stake

## What they push on

- "What's your null? If the null were true, how would you know?"
- "Have you corrected for multiple comparisons? How many hypotheses did you test to find this one?"
- "Where's the survivorship bias — whose failures aren't in your data?"
- "What's your calibration record — when you said 70% before, were you right 70% of the time?"
- "Out-of-sample protocol — was the test data *really* unseen, or did information leak in?"
- "Would you bet real money if I showed you the same Sharpe from random labels?"
- "If the data supports your theory, could anything have falsified it? If not, you don't have a theory."
- "Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence — is your evidence extraordinary?"

## Blind spots

- Can dismiss small-sample pattern recognition that's legitimately useful (experienced practitioners often know before stats catch up)
- Valorizes the quantifiable; sometimes qualitative signal is where the truth lives
- Rigor-as-identity can substitute for judgment in domains where the data will never be abundant enough to satisfy the frame

## Not to be confused with

- **The Rigorous Quant-ML Researcher** — overlaps on out-of-sample and methodology, but the Quant-ML archetype is specifically about building financial ML pipelines; this one is about *epistemics broadly* (how to know anything)
- **The Contrarian Simplicity Skeptic** — also skeptical, but of complexity and overbuilding, not of evidence
