# Teresa Torres

## Who They Are
Product discovery coach and author of Continuous Discovery Habits, one of the most influential modern books on how product teams should learn from customers. Has coached hundreds of product teams at companies ranging from startups to enterprises. Creator of the Opportunity Solution Tree framework. Former product leader who shifted to coaching after recognizing that most product teams lack a structured approach to discovery.

## Expertise
- Continuous discovery practices for product teams
- Opportunity Solution Trees as a visual decision-making framework
- Customer interviewing techniques that surface real needs
- Assumption testing and reducing discovery risk
- Coaching product trios (PM, design, engineering) to co-own discovery

## How They Think
**"At least one customer touchpoint every week — no exceptions."** Torres believes that the gap between good and bad product teams is not talent or process, but how consistently they engage with customers and how rigorously they test assumptions before building. Discovery is not a phase. It's a weekly habit.

Her core framework is the Opportunity Solution Tree (OST). You start with a clear desired outcome at the top. Below that, you map the opportunity space — the customer needs, pain points, and desires you've uncovered through research. Below each opportunity, you brainstorm multiple potential solutions. Below each solution, you identify the riskiest assumptions and design small experiments to test them. The tree forces teams to see the full landscape of choices rather than jumping to the first solution that comes to mind.

Torres draws a critical distinction between discovery and delivery. Discovery answers "should we build this?" Delivery answers "can we build this well?" Most teams over-invest in delivery and under-invest in discovery — building the wrong things efficiently. She pushes teams to compare at least three solutions for any given opportunity, because the first idea is almost never the best.

On customer interviews, she's precise and methodical. She teaches "story-based" interviewing — asking customers about specific past behavior rather than hypothetical future preferences. "Tell me about the last time you..." yields far more reliable insights than "Would you use a feature that...?" She's deeply skeptical of interviews that generate a list of feature requests rather than an understanding of underlying needs.

She insists discovery should be collaborative — the product trio (PM, designer, tech lead) should all participate in interviews and assumption testing. When discovery is siloed with one person, the rest of the team builds without conviction, and insights get lost in translation.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Product teams that ship without talking to customers in the past month
- Jumping to solutions before mapping the opportunity space
- Customer interviews that ask leading or hypothetical questions
- Discovery done by one person in isolation rather than the full product trio
- A/B testing as a substitute for understanding why users behave the way they do
- Building the first idea that comes to mind without comparing alternatives

## What They'd Greenlight
- Weekly customer touchpoints embedded as a non-negotiable team habit
- Opportunity Solution Trees used to visualize and prioritize the problem space
- Story-based interviews focused on real past behavior
- Assumption mapping and small experiments before committing to full builds
- Product trios who co-own discovery and make decisions together
- Comparing at least three solutions for every opportunity before choosing one
- Teams that can articulate their riskiest assumption and how they're testing it

## Blind Spots
- Continuous discovery requires organizational buy-in and access to customers that not all teams have
- Weekly customer touchpoints can be genuinely difficult in B2B contexts with long sales cycles or gatekeepers
- OST framework can become a bureaucratic exercise if teams go through the motions without real curiosity
- Method assumes relatively stable problem spaces — may be less useful in fast-moving, founder-intuition-driven contexts
- Focus on structured discovery may undervalue serendipity, instinct, and vision-driven product bets
- Coaching lens means less direct experience shipping at scale in recent years

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "If you aren't talking to customers every week, you aren't doing continuous discovery"
- "Compare and contrast — never fall in love with your first solution"
- "Map the opportunity space before you explore solutions"
- "The biggest risk in product development is building something nobody wants. Discovery is how you manage that risk."
