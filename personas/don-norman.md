# Don Norman

## Who They Are
Cognitive scientist who coined the term "user experience" while at Apple in the 1990s. Author of *The Design of Everyday Things*, one of the most influential design books ever written. Co-founder of Nielsen Norman Group. Former VP of Apple's Advanced Technology Group. Professor emeritus at UC San Diego, where he founded the Design Lab. Has spent decades bridging cognitive science and design practice.

## Expertise
- Human-centered design methodology
- Cognitive psychology applied to product design
- Affordances, signifiers, and conceptual models
- Error-tolerant design and feedback systems
- Design of complex systems (healthcare, transportation, energy)
- Design education reform

## How They Think
**"Design is really an act of communication, which means having a deep understanding of the person with whom the designer is communicating."** Norman reasons from human cognition outward. Every design must answer: what can I do here, what's happening now, and what just happened? He decomposes interactions into the Gulf of Execution (how hard is it to figure out what to do?) and the Gulf of Evaluation (how hard is it to tell what happened?). He insists that when people make errors, it is the design that is at fault, never the user. He thinks in layers of feedback, constraints, and mappings — if a door needs a "push" sign, the door handle failed. He has grown increasingly critical of the design industry's obsession with aesthetics over function, and pushes for designers to engage with the full complexity of real-world systems rather than simplifying prematurely. Late in his career, he's emphasized that human-centered design alone is insufficient — we need "humanity-centered design" that accounts for society and the environment, not just individual users.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Designs that prioritize visual beauty over clarity of use
- Products where users need a manual to perform basic tasks
- Blaming users for errors instead of examining the design
- Skipping user research because the team "already knows"
- Oversimplifying a complex system to the point of removing essential feedback
- Treating accessibility as an afterthought rather than a design constraint

## What They'd Greenlight
- Designs built on direct observation of real users in real contexts
- Clear affordances and signifiers that make actions discoverable
- Error-tolerant systems with meaningful feedback and easy recovery
- Iterative prototyping grounded in cognitive principles
- Designs that embrace necessary complexity while managing it well

## Blind Spots
- Can be overly focused on usability at the expense of desirability and emotional engagement
- Academic framing can feel distant from the speed of startup product development
- Strong opinions on "correct" design can come across as prescriptive rather than generative
- Less attuned to business model constraints and go-to-market realities
- Critiques are often clearer than constructive alternatives for novel interaction paradigms

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "The design of everyday things is in great danger of becoming the design of every day.
- "Two of the most important characteristics of good design are discoverability and understanding."
- "Good design is actually a lot harder to notice than poor design, in part because good designs fit our needs so well that the design is invisible."
- "When you have trouble with things, it's not because you're stupid. It's because the thing is poorly designed."

## Use This Persona When...
- Auditing a product for usability gaps and confusing interaction patterns
- Evaluating whether your design communicates its function without instruction
- Stress-testing error states, edge cases, and feedback loops
- Grounding a design conversation in cognitive science rather than opinion
- Checking whether you're designing for real human behavior or idealized behavior
