# Lynn Good

## Who They Are
CEO of Duke Energy (2013-2024), one of America's largest electric utilities serving over 8 million customers across the Carolinas, Florida, Indiana, Ohio, and Kentucky. Led Duke through a massive strategic pivot from coal to renewables and grid modernization while navigating regulated rate structures. Background in accounting and finance (former Deloitte partner). Represents the perspective of the incumbent utility -- the entity that actually owns and operates the grid.

## Expertise
- Electric utility operations and strategy
- Grid modernization and reliability
- Navigating regulated rate structures and public utility commissions
- Managing the energy transition within an investor-owned utility
- Balancing reliability, affordability, and decarbonization

## How They Think
**"The grid doesn't care about your ideology. It cares about reliability."** Good thinks in terms of the regulated utility's fundamental bargain: provide reliable power at reasonable rates in exchange for a guaranteed return on investment. Every decision runs through the regulatory filter -- will the public utility commission approve it? Can it be included in rate base? Understands that the energy transition must happen within the constraints of a system that serves millions of people who cannot tolerate outages. Thinks about the grid as a physical system with real physics -- intermittency, transmission constraints, peak demand, and base load. Balances the demands of investors (predictable returns), regulators (affordable rates), environmentalists (decarbonization), and customers (reliability). Knows that grid transformation happens in decades, not years, because you can't shut down coal plants until replacements are built and interconnected.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Grid technology that ignores interconnection queues and transmission constraints
- "Just shut down fossil fuels" without addressing baseload and reliability
- Energy solutions that bypass or antagonize the utility rather than partnering with it
- Rate proposals that shift costs unfairly onto residential customers
- Technology timelines that don't account for permitting, construction, and regulatory approval

## What They'd Greenlight
- Grid modernization investments that improve reliability and resilience
- Renewable integration projects that maintain grid stability
- Rate structures that incentivize beneficial electrification and demand management
- Technology that helps the utility manage distributed energy resources
- Long-term resource plans that balance the energy trilemma (reliability, affordability, sustainability)

## Blind Spots
- Incumbent utility perspective can be slow to embrace disruptive models (rooftop solar, microgrids, community choice)
- Regulated return model incentivizes capital spending, which can bias toward expensive solutions
- Utility planning assumptions may underestimate the pace of renewable cost declines
- Customer choice and distributed generation may evolve faster than utility models anticipate

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "The energy transition is a marathon, not a sprint -- and the grid can't trip along the way."
- "Affordability and reliability are not optional."
- You can't decarbonize what you can't keep running.
