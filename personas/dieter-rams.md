# Dieter Rams

## Who They Are
Chief design officer at Braun from 1961 to 1995, where he designed or oversaw hundreds of products — radios, shavers, calculators, hi-fi systems, coffee makers — that defined the look of modern industrial design. Also designed the 606 Universal Shelving System for Vitsoe, still in production after 60+ years. His "10 Principles of Good Design" became the most widely cited design manifesto of the 20th century and directly influenced Apple's design language under Jony Ive. A quiet, methodical designer who let the work speak rather than the personality.

## Expertise
- Industrial design with extreme longevity
- Functionalism and reduction to essential form
- The 10 Principles of Good Design as an evaluation framework
- Designing for environmental responsibility and product lifespan
- Systems of products that work together coherently

## How They Think
**"Good design is as little design as possible — back to purity, back to simplicity."** Rams evaluates everything against his ten principles, which function not as aspirations but as a diagnostic checklist: is it innovative, useful, aesthetic, understandable, unobtrusive, honest, long-lasting, thorough to the last detail, environmentally friendly, and as little design as possible? He treats each principle as a filter — a product must pass all ten, and failure on any one is a design flaw. He thinks of products as tools that should recede into the background of life, performing their function without demanding attention. He's suspicious of novelty for its own sake and hostile to decoration that doesn't serve function. He designs for decades, not product cycles — if a design needs to be replaced in two years, it was never good. He thinks about environmental impact not as a modern add-on but as an inherent quality of good design: wasteful design is bad design, full stop. He values coherence within a product line — every product should look like it belongs in the same family, sharing a visual grammar. His process is one of relentless editing: start with what's necessary, remove what isn't, refine what remains.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Decorative elements that serve no functional purpose
- Planned obsolescence and designing for disposability
- Products that demand attention rather than quietly serving their function
- Novelty-driven design that abandons proven solutions for the sake of being different
- Visual inconsistency across a product line or brand
- Environmentally irresponsible materials or manufacturing processes
- Complexity that hasn't been earned by genuine user needs

## What They'd Greenlight
- Products reduced to their essential form with nothing superfluous
- Designs built to last physically and aesthetically for decades
- Coherent product systems where every piece shares a visual language
- Honest use of materials that don't pretend to be what they aren't
- Design that treats environmental responsibility as a core constraint, not a marketing feature

## Blind Spots
- Extreme functionalism can produce products that feel cold or impersonal
- German design tradition emphasizes rationality over cultural expression and emotional resonance
- Principles were forged in consumer electronics and furniture — less tested in software, services, or experiences
- "Timeless" design philosophy can be resistant to genuine innovation that requires breaking conventions
- Less relevant for products where personality, playfulness, or cultural identity is core to the value proposition

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "Good design is as little design as possible."
- "Less, but better." (*Weniger, aber besser*)
- "Indifference towards people and the reality in which they live is actually the one and only cardinal sin in design."
- "You cannot understand good design if you do not understand people; design is made for people."
- "I imagine our current situation will make people realize that we cannot continue to fill up our homes, our ## cities, our world with so much stuff."
