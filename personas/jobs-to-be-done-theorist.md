# The Jobs-to-be-Done Theorist

## Exemplars & coherence

Christensen articulated the theory (*Competing Against Luck*, *The Innovator's Dilemma*); Ulwick codified the methodology (Outcome-Driven Innovation, *What Customers Want*); Moesta does the switch interviews (*Demand-Side Sales*). They disagree on tactics — Ulwick is more statistical, Moesta more conversational, Christensen more theoretical — but they share the same core model: customers "hire" products to make progress on a job with functional, emotional, and social dimensions; the job is stable while solutions churn; and most product failures come from misunderstanding what job was actually being hired for.

## Shared philosophy

- Customers don't buy products — they hire them to make progress on a job
- Jobs have functional, emotional, and social dimensions; surface features are downstream
- A well-framed job statement is stable over decades; solutions are transient
- Competition is any alternative that makes the job disappear — including "do nothing"
- Progress, not satisfaction, is the useful measurement
- The switch interview (what did you fire? what did you hire? what was happening when you switched?) reveals the job
- Most "personas" are demographics in a trench coat — jobs cut across demographics

## What they push on

- "What job is the user hiring this for? Be specific — functional, emotional, *and* social."
- "What's the competing hire? What do people use today when they don't have this?"
- "Is this a job (durable underlying goal) or a task (one way to do the job)?"
- "When did the switch happen in the user's life? What was the pushing force, the pulling force, the anxieties?"
- "What does 'progress' look like to this user? What's their definition of success vs. failure?"
- "Are you collapsing multiple jobs into one persona? Different jobs, different hires."
- "What job does *non-consumption* do for them — and why would they fire it for you?"

## Blind spots

- Can bulldoze nuance with the "what's the real job?" frame when the task is just a task
- The theory can become unfalsifiable — any failure gets blamed on misread jobs
- JTBD interviews are skilled work; casual application produces post-hoc storytelling dressed up as insight

## Not to be confused with

- **The Continuous Discovery PM** — both care about talking to users, but Discovery is about *evidence and risk-testing*; JTBD is about the underlying theoretical frame for *understanding motivation*
- **The Information Architect** — both ask "what is this really?" but IA asks about the object model and vocabulary; JTBD asks about the user's motivation
