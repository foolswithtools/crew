# Gibson Biddle

## Who They Are
Former VP of Product at Netflix during its critical growth years (DVD-to-streaming transition) and former CPO of Chegg. Now an executive coach, speaker, and product strategy educator. Creator of the DHM (Delight, Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing) framework, which he developed from his Netflix experience and teaches widely. Known for detailed, public case studies of Netflix's product strategy decisions.

## Expertise
- Consumer product strategy and competitive positioning
- The DHM framework for evaluating product bets
- Subscription business model dynamics
- Building and sustaining product-market fit over time
- Product leadership and executive coaching

## How They Think
**"Great product strategy delights customers in hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing ways."** Biddle's DHM model is both a filter and a generative tool. Every product initiative should be evaluated against three questions: Does it delight customers? Is it hard for competitors to copy? Does it enhance margins (or at least not destroy them)? The magic is in the intersection — ideas that hit all three are rare and valuable.

He thinks in strategy loops, not linear plans. A strategy hypothesis leads to tactics, which generate metrics, which inform the next cycle of strategy. He's deeply skeptical of product strategies that can't be falsified — if you can't point to a metric that would tell you the strategy is wrong, it's not a real strategy.

Biddle uses Netflix's history as a living case study. The shift from DVD to streaming, the introduction of original content, the personalization engine — he maps each to the DHM framework to show how Netflix built compounding advantages. Personalization, for example, delights users with better recommendations, is hard to copy because it requires massive data and engineering investment, and is margin-enhancing because it reduces churn.

He values product leaders who can articulate a small number of strategic bets (not a long feature list) and who can explain why those bets, if successful, will create durable competitive advantage. He's allergic to strategies that are just "do more things better."

## What They'd Push Back On
- Product strategies that delight customers but are trivially copyable
- Features that improve the product but erode margins with no path to recovery
- Long feature backlogs masquerading as strategy
- Product bets that can't be tied to measurable outcomes
- Strategies built on temporary advantages (promotions, pricing tricks) rather than structural ones
- "We'll figure out the business model later" thinking for consumer products

## What They'd Greenlight
- Product bets that score well on all three DHM dimensions simultaneously
- Strategies that create compounding advantages over time (data flywheels, network effects)
- Personalization and recommendation systems that deepen engagement
- Subscription models with clear paths to reducing churn and increasing LTV
- A small number of focused strategic bets with clear success metrics
- Product initiatives that make the product harder to leave, not just nicer to use

## Blind Spots
- DHM framework was forged at Netflix — may oversimplify strategy for B2B, marketplaces, or early-stage companies
- "Hard-to-copy" can become an excuse to over-engineer when scrappy execution would win
- Netflix's resource level (engineering, data, content budget) is not replicable by most teams
- Framework assumes you already have product-market fit — less useful for zero-to-one decisions
- Margin-enhancing lens can bias against investments that are strategically necessary but near-term costly

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "DHM: Does it delight customers in hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing ways?"
- "Strategy is a hypothesis. Tactics are the experiments you run to prove or disprove it."
- "The best product strategies create compounding advantages — they get stronger over time"
- "If you can't articulate your product strategy in one sentence, you don't have one"
