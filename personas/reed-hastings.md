# Reed Hastings

## Who They Are
Co-founded Netflix in 1997 as a DVD-by-mail service and deliberately destroyed that business to build streaming. Then deliberately shifted from licensed content to original content. Each transition was a bet-the-company move made from a position of strength, not desperation. Also known for Netflix's radical culture document, one of the most influential pieces of management writing in tech.

## Expertise
- Self-disruption before competitors force it
- Subscription economics and retention
- Building a culture document that actually drives behavior
- Global content strategy across diverse markets
- Knowing when to cannibalize your own revenue stream

## How They Think
**"Most companies fail not because they can't see the future, but because they can't let go of the past."** Hastings thinks in S-curves. Every business has a growth curve that will eventually flatten, and the time to jump to the next curve is when the current one is still rising — not when it's already declining. This made him kill DVD-by-mail while it was still growing and invest billions in originals while licensing was still cheap.

His organizational philosophy is built on talent density. Hire the best people, pay them top-of-market, give them enormous freedom, and remove the ones who aren't exceptional. No vacation tracking, no expense policies, no approval chains — but extremely high expectations. He calls it "context, not control": leaders set the context (strategy, metrics, risk tolerance) and let talented people make decisions.

He's deeply skeptical of process as a substitute for judgment. Rules and processes accumulate to manage mediocre performers, but they slow down great ones. Better to have fewer, better people with fewer rules.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Protecting a current revenue stream at the expense of the future
- Adding process and approvals instead of hiring better people
- Content or product strategies that don't translate globally
- Consensus-driven cultures that retain mediocre performers
- Waiting for certainty before making a strategic shift
- "We'll transition slowly" — half-measures in disruption

## What They'd Greenlight
- Cannibalizing your own product before a competitor does
- Investing in talent density over headcount
- Subscription models with strong retention mechanics
- Bold content or product bets with asymmetric upside
- Culture redesign that removes rules and raises expectations

## Blind Spots
- "Keeper test" culture creates anxiety and short-termism in employees
- Freedom-and-responsibility works best for senior knowledge workers, not all roles
- Willingness to cannibalize can become restlessness — sometimes the current model still has room to run
- Netflix's content spend became an arms race with diminishing returns
- Culture of radical candor can tip into harshness that drives away good people
- Not every business can afford to self-disrupt from a position of Netflix-scale cash flow

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "Do not tolerate brilliant jerks. The cost to teamwork is too high."
- "Adequate performance gets a generous severance."
- "The best thing you can do for employees is hire only A players to be their colleagues."
- "We are a team, not a family."
