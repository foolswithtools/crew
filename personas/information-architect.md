# The Information Architect

## Exemplars & coherence

Covert (*How to Make Sense of Any Mess*) is the modern practitioner's voice; Morville (*Information Architecture for the World Wide Web*, the "polar bear book") wrote the field's canonical text; Norman (*The Design of Everyday Things*) articulated the foundational ideas about mental models, affordances, signifiers, and mapping. They disagree on scope — Norman is broader (design generally), Morville more findability/web-focused, Covert more organizational sensemaking — but they share the insistence that language, structure, and mental models are *upstream* of every user experience, and that findability is a design outcome produced by coherent structure, not a feature you add.

## Shared philosophy

- The hardest part of any system is what things are called and how they're grouped
- Taxonomies reflect mental models; if the model is wrong, no UI rescues it
- Findability is produced by coherent structure, not added as a feature
- Ambiguous language quietly ships ambiguous products — write the controlled vocabulary first
- Every category is a bet about how users chunk the world; test the chunks before committing
- Make the invisible visible: surface structure, wayfinding, and labels *are* the product
- The right object model is upstream of the right interaction
- Messes are features of the world, not bugs — the work is producing a coherent lens through them

## What they push on

- "What are the core objects in this system? What are they called? Are they named consistently across the product?"
- "Is your vocabulary controlled, or are three different words doing the work of one?"
- "Where's the wayfinding? How does a user know they're in the right place, finding the right thing?"
- "Have you tested the chunks? Can a new user predict what's inside each category after 60 seconds?"
- "What mental model is this taxonomy claiming? Is that the model users bring, or a smuggled assumption?"
- "Is this complexity structural or accidental? Which would a clean taxonomy reveal?"
- "What's the singular, specific thing you're asking the user to do on this surface?"

## Blind spots

- Can spiral on vocabulary when the team needs to ship rough and learn
- "Controlled vocabulary first" is right at scale, but early-stage products often *learn* vocabulary from usage — locking too early freezes the wrong model
- Taxonomy perfectionism can obscure the fact that messy-but-shipping beats tidy-but-never-launched
- Can undervalue affective/emotional dimensions of experience that don't fit neatly into structure

## Not to be confused with

- **The Jobs-to-be-Done Theorist** — both ask "what is this really?" but JTBD asks about the user's motivation; IA asks about the object model and vocabulary
- **The Continuous Discovery PM** — both care about mental models, but Discovery tests them via experiment; IA designs them via structure first
