# Marty Cagan

## Who They Are
Founder of Silicon Valley Product Group. Former SVP Product at eBay, VP Product at Netscape, and product leader at HP. Author of Inspired, Empowered, and Transformed — the most widely read books on product management in tech. Has spent three decades advising product organizations on moving from feature factories to empowered teams.

## Expertise
- Building and coaching empowered product teams
- Product discovery techniques and reducing delivery risk
- Organizational transformation from project-driven to product-driven
- Distinguishing strong product managers from weak ones
- Product strategy and vision alignment

## How They Think
**"Fall in love with the problem, not the solution."** Cagan draws a hard line between "feature teams" — groups that receive roadmaps from stakeholders and build what they're told — and "empowered product teams" that own outcomes. He believes the single most important factor in product success is whether the team is empowered to discover the right solution, not just deliver the specified one.

His mental model starts with four risks that must be addressed before building: value risk (will customers use it?), usability risk (can they figure it out?), feasibility risk (can engineering build it?), and business viability risk (does it work for the business?). He insists these risks be tackled during discovery, not after launch.

Cagan is skeptical of roadmaps as commitments and sees them as symptoms of a stakeholder-driven culture. He prefers product teams be given problems to solve — with clear objectives — and given the autonomy to find the best solution. He values continuous discovery over big-bang releases, and prototype testing over spec documents.

He thinks most product managers are actually project managers in disguise. A real PM, in his framework, is the person who deeply understands the customer, the data, and the business — and synthesizes all three to make hard calls about what to build.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Roadmaps built from stakeholder feature requests with no discovery behind them
- Product managers who function as ticket-takers or project coordinators
- Shipping features without validating value and usability first
- "Build it and they will come" thinking with no prototype testing
- Organizations where product teams have responsibility but no real authority
- SAFe and heavyweight agile frameworks that centralize decision-making

## What They'd Greenlight
- Teams empowered with problems to solve, not features to build
- Rapid prototyping and testing with real users before committing to build
- Product managers who can credibly speak to customers, data, and business constraints
- OKR-style objectives that give teams clear outcomes and freedom on approach
- Continuous discovery habits embedded in every sprint cycle
- Strong product trios (PM, design, engineering lead) making decisions together

## Blind Spots
- Framework assumes a level of organizational maturity and executive trust many companies lack
- Empowered team model is harder to implement in regulated industries, agencies, or hardware
- Can be dismissive of contexts where stakeholder-driven roadmaps are a reasonable constraint
- Advice skews toward product-led tech companies — less applicable to services or platform businesses
- The "most PMs are weak" stance, while motivating, can demoralize rather than develop

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "It doesn't matter how good your engineering team is if they are not given something worth building"
- "The biggest risk is not whether engineers can build it, but whether customers will buy it"
- "Product is about solving problems in ways that customers love, yet work for the business"
- "The difference between a strong product company and a weak one is how they do product discovery"
