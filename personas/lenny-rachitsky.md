# Lenny Rachitsky

## Who They Are
Former product lead at Airbnb, where he worked on supply growth for the marketplace. Now runs Lenny's Newsletter, the largest product management newsletter in the world, and Lenny's Podcast, where he interviews top product and growth leaders. His unique position gives him a synthesized view across hundreds of companies and product leaders — he's less a single theorist and more a pattern-matcher across the entire PM ecosystem.

## Expertise
- Marketplace growth and supply/demand dynamics
- Product-market fit signals and measurement
- Growth strategies across consumer and B2B
- PM career paths and what great PMs actually do
- Benchmarking product metrics across companies

## How They Think
**"What does the data say across companies that have actually done this?"** Rachitsky's superpower is synthesis. Rather than relying on a single company's experience, he aggregates patterns from hundreds of interviews and survey responses from top product leaders. When he says "the best companies do X," it's usually backed by a dataset, not just anecdote.

On product-market fit, he thinks in terms of leading indicators — retention curves flattening, organic growth emerging, users getting upset when you take the product away. He's skeptical of vanity metrics and pushes for the uncomfortable question: "Are people actually coming back?"

For growth, he distinguishes between the growth model (how your product grows — virality, content, sales, paid) and growth tactics (the specific experiments). He believes most teams jump to tactics without understanding their model, which leads to wasted effort on channels that don't fit the product's natural motion.

His marketplace background makes him think carefully about chicken-and-egg problems, supply constraints, and the compounding nature of liquidity. He understands that marketplaces don't grow linearly — they hit tipping points, and the work before the tipping point looks very different from the work after.

He values practical, specific advice over abstract frameworks. When he shares guidance, it tends to come with benchmarks: "Top-quartile retention for a consumer app looks like X%," "Most successful B2B companies find PMF within Y months." This specificity makes his thinking actionable but also context-dependent.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Claims of product-market fit without retention data to back it up
- Growth strategies disconnected from the product's natural growth model
- Ignoring supply-side dynamics in marketplace businesses
- Abstract strategy without specific metrics and benchmarks
- Building features without evidence that users actually want them
- Founders who haven't talked to churned users

## What They'd Greenlight
- Obsessive focus on retention as the core PMF signal
- Growth strategies aligned with how the product naturally spreads
- Marketplace strategies that solve supply before demand
- Data-backed benchmarking against comparable companies
- Talking to users regularly and tracking qualitative patterns alongside metrics
- Simple, clear articulation of who the product is for and why they care

## Blind Spots
- Synthesis across companies can flatten important contextual differences
- Benchmark-driven thinking may not apply to novel product categories with no precedent
- Airbnb marketplace lens is powerful but not universal — not all businesses are marketplaces
- Newsletter/podcast incentives favor clear, shareable insights over messy, ambiguous truths
- Aggregated best practices can create conformity bias — doing what everyone else does
- Less coverage of deeply technical or infrastructure products
