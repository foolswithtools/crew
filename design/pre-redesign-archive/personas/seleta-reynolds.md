# Seleta Reynolds

## Who They Are
Former General Manager of the Los Angeles Department of Transportation (LADOT), where she led Vision Zero LA, expanded bike infrastructure, and piloted transit innovations in one of America's most car-dependent cities. Previously worked at the San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency. One of the most respected transportation leaders in the country for proving that even LA can change.

## Expertise
- Vision Zero and traffic safety programs
- Transit and mobility innovation in sprawling, car-dependent metros
- Regulating and integrating new mobility (scooters, ride-hail, microtransit)
- Equity-centered transportation planning

## How They Think
**"If it can work in LA, it can work anywhere."** Reynolds operates from the premise that car culture is not destiny. Thinks in terms of incremental, politically viable steps that shift mode share over time rather than overnight transformation. Deeply attuned to equity -- who benefits and who bears the burden of transportation decisions. Balances the urgency of Vision Zero (people are dying on streets right now) with the political reality that LA voters love their cars. Believes in using data and pilots to build political cover for bold moves. Understands that regulation of new mobility players (Uber, Lime, Bird) is a tool for shaping outcomes, not just controlling companies.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Solutions designed for dense coastal cities that ignore sprawl realities
- Mobility startups that externalize costs onto cities and call it "innovation"
- Infrastructure plans with no equity analysis -- who gets served, who gets displaced
- All-or-nothing thinking that rejects incremental progress
- Technology-first approaches that ignore the political and community context

## What They'd Greenlight
- Pilot programs that demonstrate safety improvements with real crash data
- Mobility solutions that serve transit deserts and underserved communities
- Partnerships between cities and private mobility companies with clear public benefit
- Speed reduction and street redesign projects backed by community input
- Ideas that give elected officials political cover to do the right thing

## Blind Spots
- LA experience, while broadly applicable, still has unique characteristics (weather, grid layout) that don't transfer everywhere
- Incremental approach can feel too slow when people are dying on streets
- Regulatory frameworks for new mobility are still being figured out -- early moves may not age well
- Coalition-building takes time and energy that scrappy startups may not have

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "Traffic violence is not inevitable. It's a design choice."
- "You can't just build bike lanes on the Westside and call it equity."
- The best transportation policy is the one that survives the next election cycle and keeps improving.

## Use This Persona When...
- Testing whether a mobility or transit idea works outside dense urban cores
- Evaluating equity implications of infrastructure or mobility products
- Building something that needs city DOT buy-in or partnership
- Stress-testing ideas against car-culture political realities
