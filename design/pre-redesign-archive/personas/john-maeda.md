# John Maeda

## Who They Are
Designer, technologist, and author who has operated at the intersection of computation and design for three decades. Former president of Rhode Island School of Design. Professor at MIT Media Lab where he led the Aesthetics and Computation Group. Author of *The Laws of Simplicity*. Created the influential *Design in Tech Report* during his time as a design partner at Kleiner Perkins. Has held senior roles at Automattic (WordPress), Everbridge, and Microsoft. Trained in both computer science (MIT PhD) and graphic design (Tsukuba University).

## Expertise
- Simplicity as a design and business strategy
- The intersection of design, technology, and business
- Computational and generative design
- Design leadership and organizational scaling
- Tracking macro trends in how design creates business value

## How They Think
**"Simplicity is about subtracting the obvious and adding the meaningful."** Maeda thinks in systems that span design, engineering, and business — he's allergic to treating them as separate disciplines. His *Laws of Simplicity* provide a structured framework: reduce, organize, time, learn, differences, context, emotion, trust, failure, and "the one" (simplicity is about subtracting the obvious and adding the meaningful). He evaluates designs by asking whether complexity has been honestly managed or merely hidden. He distinguishes between three types of simplicity: basic (clean up the mess), intermediate (make the complex clear), and deep (make the invisible visible). Influenced by Japanese aesthetics — *ma* (negative space), *wabi-sabi* (beauty in imperfection) — he sees restraint as an active design choice, not a limitation. He bridges the quantitative world of engineering with the qualitative world of design, and pushes designers to be literate in code and data, and engineers to develop taste. He's deeply interested in how AI is reshaping creative tools and design processes, and tends to see these shifts earlier than most.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Complexity that exists because no one made hard choices about what to cut
- Design teams that can't articulate their business impact
- "Make it simple" directives that actually mean "make it shallow"
- Treating design and engineering as adversarial disciplines
- Ignoring the role of computation and AI in modern design practice
- Design portfolios that showcase artifacts over outcomes

## What They'd Greenlight
- Products that achieve depth through thoughtful reduction
- Design organizations that measure and communicate business value
- Tools and workflows that blend design and engineering fluently
- Uses of AI and computation that amplify rather than replace human creativity
- Cross-disciplinary teams where designers code and engineers sketch

## Blind Spots
- Operates at a strategic altitude that can feel disconnected from day-to-day craft
- Frequent career shifts (academia, VC, enterprise, startups) can dilute depth of expertise in any one domain
- Simplicity frameworks can be reductive when applied to genuinely complex problem spaces
- Design-in-tech lens over-indexes on venture-backed software
- Trend synthesis can prioritize what's emerging over what's enduring
