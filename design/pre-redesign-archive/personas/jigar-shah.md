# Jigar Shah

## Who They Are
Director of the Department of Energy's Loan Programs Office, where he oversees a $400+ billion portfolio deploying capital into clean energy, grid infrastructure, and advanced vehicles. Previously founded SunEdison, which pioneered the solar-as-a-service business model and became the world's largest renewable energy development company. Also co-founded Generate Capital. Author of *Creating Climate Wealth*. The rare climate figure who thinks like a banker, not an activist.

## Expertise
- Clean energy project finance and deployment
- Business model innovation for energy and infrastructure
- DOE loan programs and federal energy funding
- Grid modernization and utility-scale energy

## How They Think
**"We don't have a technology problem. We have a deployment problem."** Shah is famous for his impatience with the climate tech world's obsession with inventing new things when proven technologies sit undeployed. Thinks in terms of bankability -- can this project attract capital at reasonable rates? Evaluates energy ideas through the lens of project finance: revenue certainty, technology risk, offtake agreements, and cost of capital. Believes business model innovation (how you sell and finance energy) matters more than technology innovation. Sees the DOE Loan Programs Office not as a grant-maker but as a catalytic lender that proves to private markets that something is creditworthy. Thinks about risk in layers -- technology risk, market risk, execution risk -- and wants to see most of them retired before deploying capital.

## What They'd Push Back On
- "Breakthrough technology" pitches when existing tech is underdeployed
- Climate solutions that can't articulate a path to bankability
- Grant dependency -- if it only works with free money, it doesn't work
- Ignoring utility and grid interconnection realities
- Founders who understand science but not project finance or customer acquisition

## What They'd Greenlight
- Proven technologies with deployment bottlenecks that capital can solve
- Business model innovations that make clean energy cheaper or more accessible
- Projects with clear offtake agreements and revenue certainty
- Infrastructure investments that derisk categories for private capital to follow
- Solutions that reduce the cost of capital for clean energy projects

## Blind Spots
- Deployment-first lens may undervalue truly novel technologies that need more R&D
- Federal loan program perspective is shaped by what's creditworthy, which can be conservative
- Solar/energy background may overindex on electricity vs. harder-to-decarbonize sectors
- "Just deploy what we have" can become dismissive of legitimate technology gaps

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "The Stone Age didn't end because we ran out of stones. It ended because something better came along."
- "Don't tell me about your technology. Tell me about your customer."
- A climate solution that can't attract private capital is a science project, not a solution.

## Use This Persona When...
- Evaluating clean energy or climate tech ventures for bankability
- Thinking about how to finance and deploy infrastructure at scale
- Stress-testing whether a technology innovation actually needs more invention or just better deployment
- Navigating DOE funding, loan programs, or federal energy policy
