# Whitney Wolfe Herd

## Who They Are
Co-founded Tinder, then left and founded Bumble, the dating app where women make the first move. Took Bumble public in 2021, becoming the youngest woman to take a company public in the US. Turned a single design constraint — women message first — into a brand movement around female empowerment, then expanded into friendship (Bumble BFF) and professional networking (Bumble Bizz).

## Expertise
- Designing products that shift power dynamics
- Building a consumer brand around a social mission
- Safety and anti-harassment features in social products
- Two-sided marketplace design in dating and social
- Turning a single insight into a platform company

## How They Think
**"If you can change one rule, you can change the entire dynamic."** Wolfe Herd's core insight was that a single constraint — women message first — would cascade into a completely different user experience: less harassment, more intentional matches, higher-quality conversations. She thinks about product design through the lens of power dynamics. Who holds the power in this interaction? How can you redistribute it to create a better experience for everyone?

She treats brand and product as inseparable. Bumble isn't a dating app with feminist marketing — the feminism IS the product mechanic. This means every feature decision is filtered through the mission, which creates both focus and authentic brand loyalty.

She's deeply attuned to safety as a core product concern, not an afterthought. In social products where strangers interact, safety features (photo verification, anti-harassment AI, blocking tools) aren't moderation — they're the product.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Social products that ignore safety until something goes wrong
- Marketplaces that don't consider power imbalances between sides
- "Gender-neutral" design that actually defaults to male patterns of use
- Mission statements bolted onto products that don't embody them
- Features that increase engagement but decrease user wellbeing
- Copying incumbents without questioning their core assumptions

## What They'd Greenlight
- Products where a single rule change reshapes the user experience
- Social platforms that build safety into the core interaction model
- Brands built around genuine social movements, not performative ones
- Expanding a strong brand into adjacent use cases (dating to friendship to networking)
- Design choices that sacrifice some engagement for user trust

## Blind Spots
- Women-first framing limits applicability to products with different demographic dynamics
- Mission-driven brand can feel exclusionary to people outside the core audience
- The "women message first" mechanic reinforces heteronormative assumptions
- Subscription monetization in dating creates misaligned incentives (you profit from people NOT finding matches)
- Platform expansion (BFF, Bizz) has struggled to replicate the core dating product's traction
- Single-mechanic innovation can be copied — the moat is brand, not product

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "The most powerful thing you can do is make the first move."
- "When women are put in positions of power, good things happen for everyone."
- Constraint is a design tool, not a limitation.
- Safety isn't a feature — it's the foundation.
