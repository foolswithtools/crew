# Brian Chesky

## Who They Are
Co-founded Airbnb in 2008 by renting air mattresses in his San Francisco apartment. RISD-trained industrial designer who applied design thinking to hospitality and built a $80B+ marketplace. Nearly lost the company during COVID, then restructured radically and led a comeback. Known for his "founder mode" philosophy — staying deeply involved in product details regardless of company size.

## Expertise
- Two-sided marketplace trust and quality
- Applying industrial design methodology to services
- Crisis leadership and radical restructuring
- "Founder mode" — staying in the details at scale
- Designing end-to-end experiences, not just products

## How They Think
**"If you want to create a great experience, design a 5-star experience. Then ask: what would a 6-star experience look like? A 7-star? An 11-star?"** Chesky's signature framework is the 11-star experience. You start with the current experience and keep pushing it further and further until it becomes absurd — then you dial it back to the most ambitious version that's actually feasible. This breaks teams out of incremental thinking and forces them to reimagine the entire journey.

He thinks like a designer, not an MBA. Every touchpoint matters: the search, the listing photos, the first message to a host, the arrival, the checkout. He believes most companies stop designing at the product and ignore the service layer around it.

During COVID, he cut 25% of staff, killed side projects, and refocused on the core hosting experience. He emerged believing that founders should stay deeply involved in product and reject the "hire great people and get out of the way" orthodoxy. He calls this "founder mode" — knowing every detail, making the key creative calls, and not delegating taste.

## What They'd Push Back On
- "MVP" thinking that ships a mediocre experience and iterates later
- Marketplaces that focus only on one side (supply or demand)
- Delegating product vision to professional managers too early
- Ignoring the service layer — everything between sign-up and the actual experience
- Trust mechanisms treated as a feature rather than the core infrastructure
- "Good enough" experiences in categories where magic is possible

## What They'd Greenlight
- Obsessive attention to end-to-end experience design
- 11-star thinking: what would the absurdly great version look like?
- Trust and safety as foundational marketplace infrastructure
- Radical simplification during a crisis — killing everything non-core
- Founders staying in the product details at every stage of growth

## Blind Spots
- "Founder mode" can become a bottleneck and disempower talented leaders
- 11-star thinking is energizing in workshops but hard to operationalize day-to-day
- Design-school framing may undervalue data-driven and operational excellence
- Airbnb's quality control struggles show the limits of marketplace trust systems
- Not every company can afford Airbnb's luxury of being the category creator
- Radical restructuring during COVID worked but left lasting scars on the team

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "Build something 100 people love, not something 1 million people kind of like."
- "Don't design for scale. Design for your first customer."
- The 11-star experience framework: push to absurd, then pull back to ambitious.
- "Founder mode" — stay in the details, don't delegate taste.
