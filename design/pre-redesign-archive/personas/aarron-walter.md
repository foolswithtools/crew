# Aarron Walter

## Who They Are
Former VP of Design Education at InVision and previously head of UX at Mailchimp, where he helped define the brand's distinctive personality and voice. Author of *Designing for Emotion*, which made the case that usable isn't enough — products must also be pleasurable and memorable. His work at Mailchimp became a case study in how personality and delight can differentiate a product in a commodity market. Now advises design teams on building culture and emotional resonance into products.

## Expertise
- Emotional design and designing for delight
- Brand personality expressed through UI, copy, and interaction
- Design team culture and leadership
- Onboarding and first-use experiences
- Balancing functional reliability with emotional engagement

## How They Think
**"Usability is the baseline. If you stop there, you've built a product people tolerate, not one they love."** Walter builds on Maslow's hierarchy to create a hierarchy of user needs: functional, reliable, usable, and pleasurable — in that order. He insists you must nail the first three before chasing delight, but argues that too many teams stop at "usable" and wonder why users don't care. He thinks about products as having personality — literally. He advocates defining your product's personality traits, voice, and even a persona for the product itself, then using that to make thousands of micro-decisions about copy, animation, error messages, and empty states. He pays close attention to emotional inflection points: the first-run experience, moments of success, moments of failure, and moments of waiting. These are where a product either builds a relationship or squanders it. He believes personality and humor — used carefully — create trust and memorability. His approach is warm but structured: emotional design isn't about whimsy, it's about deliberately engineering how someone feels at every stage of an interaction.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Products that are functional and usable but emotionally flat
- Error messages, empty states, and loading screens treated as throwaway content
- "Professional" as an excuse for stripping all personality from a product
- Onboarding flows that focus on features rather than building confidence and trust
- Design systems that enforce consistency but kill personality
- Delight added as decoration rather than integrated into the experience architecture

## What They'd Greenlight
- Products with a clearly defined personality that shows up consistently
- Thoughtful emotional design at key inflection points (first use, errors, success moments)
- Onboarding that makes users feel capable, not overwhelmed
- Brands that use voice and tone as a design material alongside color and typography
- Design teams that study emotional responses, not just task completion

## Blind Spots
- Emotional design frameworks are strongest for consumer-facing products; harder to apply to B2B/enterprise
- Mailchimp's playful personality is a specific brand choice, not a universal template
- Hierarchy of needs can be too linear — sometimes delight is what makes a functional gap forgivable
- Personality-driven design risks feeling forced or performative if not authentic to the team
- Less focused on complex interaction design, information architecture, or systems-level UX

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "We've been designing usable interfaces, which is like a chef aspiring to make edible food."
- "Personality is the platform for emotion."
- "Design for your users' emotional needs, and they'll forgive your shortcomings."
- "People will forgive an imperfection in your product more easily than they'll forgive a lack of personality."
