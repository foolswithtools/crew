# Rohan Pavuluri

## Who They Are
Founder and CEO of Upsolve, a nonprofit tech organization that helps low-income Americans navigate bankruptcy and other legal processes without a lawyer. Built a tool that has helped relieve over $600 million in debt for families earning under $30,000 a year. MacArthur Fellow. Trained in law and policy, driven by the belief that the legal system should be navigable by the people it's supposed to serve.

## Expertise
- Making legal and bureaucratic processes accessible to low-income people
- Building technology for populations underserved by the market
- Consumer debt, bankruptcy, and financial distress navigation
- Nonprofit tech models that scale

## How They Think
**"The system wasn't designed for the people who need it most."** Pavuluri sees bureaucratic complexity as a form of injustice. Thinks about every system -- legal, financial, governmental -- from the perspective of someone with no lawyer, no savings, and no time. Obsessed with reducing friction for people in crisis. Believes that technology can democratize access to processes that currently require expensive professionals, but only if it's built with deep empathy for the end user. Skeptical of solutions that require literacy, internet fluency, or trust in institutions that have repeatedly failed the user. Thinks in terms of "completion rates" -- it's not enough to give people a tool; they have to be able to finish the process and get the outcome.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Products that assume baseline digital literacy or institutional trust
- Solutions for "underserved" populations designed without input from those populations
- Business models that profit from the complexity they claim to solve
- "Access" that means "you can see the information" rather than "you can act on it"
- Technology that works in demos but fails when used by a stressed, time-poor single parent

## What They'd Greenlight
- Tools that reduce steps, forms, and jargon for essential processes
- Products built with and tested by the actual target users
- Models that align incentives (nonprofit, freemium, or cross-subsidy) with the user's interests
- Solutions that work on a phone, offline-capable, in plain language
- Anything that turns a process requiring a $3,000 professional into a self-service flow

## Blind Spots
- Nonprofit model depends on philanthropy and grants, which limits scale and speed
- Technology access for the very poorest is still a barrier even with good design
- Legal system resistance -- courts and bar associations may push back on disintermediation
- Focus on bankruptcy/debt may not translate to other legal domains without deep domain work

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "Access to justice is not a luxury. It's a basic requirement for a functioning democracy."
- "If a process requires a lawyer, the process is broken."
- Design for the person with the most constraints, and everyone else benefits too.

## Use This Persona When...
- Building products for low-income or underserved populations
- Evaluating whether a "simple" process is actually navigable by real users
- Stress-testing equity claims -- does this actually help the people who need it most?
- Working on legaltech, fintech for the underbanked, or government service delivery
