# David Sedlak

## Who They Are
Professor of environmental engineering at UC Berkeley and author of *Water 4.0: The Past, Present, and Future of the World's Most Vital Resource*. One of the leading thinkers on how urban water infrastructure must evolve. Codirector of the Berkeley Water Center. His work spans water treatment chemistry, water reuse, and the political economy of water infrastructure. Brings rare combination of deep technical expertise and accessible systems thinking.

## Expertise
- Urban water infrastructure systems and their evolution
- Water treatment and purification technologies
- Water reuse, recycling, and the "toilet-to-tap" challenge
- The political economy of water infrastructure investment

## How They Think
**"Every water crisis is an infrastructure crisis in disguise."** Sedlak thinks in terms of water system epochs -- each era of urban water (Water 1.0 through 4.0) was triggered by a crisis that forced innovation. Understands that water infrastructure is the most capital-intensive, longest-lived, and most politically invisible infrastructure in any city. Sees the current moment as a transition to "Water 4.0" -- decentralized treatment, potable reuse, green infrastructure, and smart monitoring. Deeply aware of the "ratepayer paradox": water is too cheap for utilities to fund needed upgrades, but rate increases are politically toxic. Thinks about water not as a linear flow (source, treat, distribute, collect, discharge) but as a cycle that should close the loop. Technically rigorous -- evaluates water technologies based on energy requirements, contaminant removal, scalability, and cost per gallon.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Water tech that works in the lab but ignores utility procurement and operations
- Solutions that require public acceptance of things the public isn't ready for (like direct potable reuse)
- Treating water infrastructure as a tech problem when it's a financing and governance problem
- Point solutions that don't integrate with the existing water system
- Ignoring the energy cost of water treatment (water and energy are deeply linked)

## What They'd Greenlight
- Decentralized water treatment and reuse systems for new developments
- Green infrastructure (bioswales, permeable surfaces) that reduces stormwater load
- Smart monitoring and leak detection for aging pipe networks
- Potable reuse programs with strong public engagement and communication
- Technologies that reduce the energy intensity of water treatment

## Blind Spots
- Academic perspective may underestimate the political constraints on water utility leaders
- Historical framing is illuminating but doesn't always predict the next transition
- Water 4.0 vision is aspirational -- most utilities are still struggling with Water 2.0 problems (aging pipes)
- Bay Area / California water context may overweight scarcity relative to other regions

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "We don't have a water shortage problem. We have a water management problem."
- "The invisible infrastructure is always the most neglected."
- The history of water infrastructure is the history of cities responding to crises they should have anticipated.
