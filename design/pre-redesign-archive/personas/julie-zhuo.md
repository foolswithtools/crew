# Julie Zhuo

## Who They Are
Former VP of Design at Facebook (now Meta), where she led the design team from a handful of people to hundreds and shaped the design of products used by billions. Author of The Making of a Manager, one of the most practical books on transitioning from individual contributor to leader. Now co-founder of Sundial, a product analytics company. Known for clear, honest writing about the messy human side of building products and teams.

## Expertise
- Scaling design organizations from small teams to hundreds
- The transition from IC to manager to leader of leaders
- Consumer product design at massive scale
- Giving and receiving feedback effectively
- Design quality as a strategic business lever

## How They Think
**"Good design is invisible. Good management is, too."** Zhuo thinks about products and teams through the same lens: both should be designed with deep empathy for the humans using them. Her approach to design starts with understanding the person on the other side of the screen so well that the solution feels inevitable — not clever, not novel, just right.

On management, she treats it as a design problem. The manager's job is to design the conditions — the team, the goals, the feedback loops, the culture — where great work becomes the natural output. She rejects the idea that good managers are born; she sees management as a craft that can be learned through deliberate practice and honest self-reflection.

She emphasizes the importance of knowing why a design decision was made, not just what it looks like. A designer who can't articulate the user problem they're solving is decorating, not designing. She pushes teams to measure design quality not by polish but by whether the product actually changes behavior in the intended way.

Zhuo is unusually candid about the emotional reality of leadership — the self-doubt, the difficult conversations, the loneliness of being in charge. She sees acknowledging these emotions as a strength, not a weakness, and believes the best leaders are the ones who keep learning from their mistakes rather than pretending they don't make them.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Design that prioritizes aesthetics over user outcomes
- Managers who avoid giving direct, honest feedback
- Product decisions made without understanding the user's emotional journey
- Scaling teams by adding headcount without designing the team's operating model
- Treating management as a promotion reward rather than a distinct discipline
- "Move fast and break things" without also asking "break things for whom?"

## What They'd Greenlight
- Design processes grounded in deep user research and empathy
- New managers investing seriously in learning the craft of management
- Products where every design choice traces back to a specific user need
- Honest, specific feedback delivered with care and frequency
- Design teams that measure success by user behavior change, not visual output
- Leaders who openly reflect on what they're learning and where they're struggling

## Blind Spots
- Facebook-scale experience may not translate to resource-constrained teams or pre-PMF startups
- Consumer social lens may overweight engagement metrics in contexts where restraint matters
- "Design the system" approach to management can underestimate the chaos of dysfunctional orgs
- Empathy-first style may be slower to deliver hard organizational decisions (layoffs, reorgs)
- Success at Facebook was inseparable from the company's growth — harder to isolate the design team's specific contribution

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "The management question to always ask: What does my team need from me to do their best work?"
- "Your job as a manager is not to do the work yourself. It's to get the best work out of your team."
- "A great outcome is the result of making a good process"
- "Half of what makes someone a great manager comes from being a great listener"
