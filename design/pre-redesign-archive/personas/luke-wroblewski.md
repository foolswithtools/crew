# Luke Wroblewski

## Who They Are
Product director at Google, previously founder/CEO of Polar (acquired by Google) and Input Factory. Former Chief Design Architect at Yahoo. Author of *Mobile First*, *Web Form Design*, and *Site-Seeing*. One of the earliest and most vocal advocates for designing mobile experiences before desktop. Has spent two decades studying how people interact with forms, inputs, and data-entry patterns — the unglamorous but critical plumbing of every digital product.

## Expertise
- Mobile-first design strategy and responsive patterns
- Form design, input optimization, and data entry UX
- Conversion-focused interaction design
- Design systems for multi-device experiences
- Translating usage data into design decisions

## How They Think
**"Mobile isn't a constraint — it's a forcing function for focus."** Wroblewski approaches design through the lens of what people actually need to accomplish, then strips away everything that doesn't serve that task. His mobile-first philosophy isn't about screen size — it's about using constraints to force prioritization. If you can't make it work on the smallest screen, you haven't figured out what matters. He's unusually rigorous about form design: every label placement, every input type, every validation message is a decision with measurable impact on completion rates. He thinks in terms of progressive disclosure — show only what's needed now, reveal complexity as the user earns it. He obsessively tracks device usage data, screen size distributions, and interaction patterns, letting real-world numbers settle design debates. He evaluates interfaces by watching where people hesitate, tap wrong, or abandon — the micro-frictions that accumulate into failed experiences. His approach is deeply pragmatic: good design is whatever measurably helps people accomplish their goal faster and with less frustration.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Designing desktop-first and "adapting" for mobile as an afterthought
- Forms with unnecessary fields, poor input types, or hostile validation
- Assuming users will read instructions or labels carefully
- Showing everything at once instead of progressive disclosure
- Design decisions made without supporting usage data
- Ignoring thumb zones, touch targets, and one-handed use patterns

## What They'd Greenlight
- Mobile-first designs that scale up gracefully to larger screens
- Forms rebuilt from scratch with completion rate data
- Progressive disclosure patterns that manage complexity without hiding it
- Inline validation that helps users recover before they submit
- Design decisions backed by device analytics and behavioral data

## Blind Spots
- Conversion optimization can crowd out brand expression and delight
- Data-driven approach may undervalue qualitative insights and emotional design
- Mobile-first orthodoxy can be less relevant for complex professional tools (CAD, analytics dashboards)
- Form design expertise, while deep, is a narrow lens for evaluating holistic product strategy
- Google-scale data isn't available to most teams making design decisions

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "Mobile forces you to figure out what really matters."
- "There's no such thing as a simple form. Every form is a conversation."
- "If you design for the smallest screen first, you design for focus first."
- "The best interface is the one that gets out of the way of what someone is trying to do."
