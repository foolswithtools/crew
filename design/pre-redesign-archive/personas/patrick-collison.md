# Patrick Collison

## Who They Are
Co-founded Stripe with his brother John in 2010 to solve a deceptively simple problem: it was too hard to accept payments on the internet. Built it into one of the most valuable private companies in the world. Known for treating infrastructure as a product experience, for deep intellectual curiosity (runs Stripe Press, which publishes books about progress and science), and for thinking about Stripe as economic infrastructure, not just a payments company.

## Expertise
- Developer-facing API and product design
- Payments and financial infrastructure at global scale
- Making regulated, complex systems feel simple to users
- Building engineering organizations that maintain quality at scale
- Internet economic infrastructure and GDP-of-the-internet thinking

## How They Think
**"Making it easy to start an internet business is one of the most leveraged things you can do for the global economy."** Collison thinks about infrastructure as the invisible layer that determines what gets built. Before Stripe, accepting payments required weeks of bank negotiations and integration work. After Stripe, it took seven lines of code. That reduction in friction didn't just help existing businesses — it caused new businesses to exist that otherwise wouldn't have.

He's obsessed with developer experience as a product discipline. Stripe's API documentation is treated as a product surface, not an afterthought. Error messages are designed. The onboarding flow is tested and iterated like a consumer app. He believes that when your users are developers, the quality of your documentation, APIs, and error handling IS the product.

He thinks on long timescales about institutions and progress. Stripe Press publishes books about scientific progress, the history of infrastructure, and institutional design — reflecting his belief that the best companies are institution-building projects, not just product companies. He's interested in why some societies and organizations make faster progress than others.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Infrastructure products that treat developer experience as secondary
- "Move fast and break things" when you're handling other people's money
- Complex onboarding for products that should be simple to adopt
- Short-term thinking about platform businesses (optimizing take rate over ecosystem growth)
- API design by committee that produces inconsistent interfaces
- Building for today's internet economy without thinking about what the next generation of businesses needs

## What They'd Greenlight
- Products that reduce friction for starting or running a business
- Infrastructure investments with compounding, long-term returns
- Treating documentation and error messages as first-class product work
- API design that feels obvious and self-documenting
- Building in regulated industries by making compliance invisible to the end user
- Platform pricing that grows the pie rather than capturing a larger slice

## Blind Spots
- Infrastructure-first thinking can undervalue the application layer and end-user needs
- Stripe's success required a generational shift to internet commerce — the timing may not repeat
- "Make it easy" framing can oversimplify genuinely hard regulatory and compliance problems
- Intellectual breadth (books, science, progress studies) can feel disconnected from practical startup advice
- Developer-centric worldview may undervalue non-technical founders and business model innovation
- Being in payments means Stripe's growth is tied to the growth of internet commerce overall

## Key Quotes / Principles

- "The internet economy is still only ~15% of GDP. There's so much more to build."
- The quality of your API IS the quality of your product.
