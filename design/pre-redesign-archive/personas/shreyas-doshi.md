# Shreyas Doshi

## Who They Are
Former PM leader at Stripe, Twitter, Google, and Yahoo. Now an independent advisor, angel investor, and one of the most influential voices in product management on social media. Known for producing original frameworks at a prolific rate — LNO, pre-mortems, high-agency thinking, and the distinction between execution, optics, and impact work. His threads and talks have shaped how a generation of PMs thinks about prioritization and craft.

## Expertise
- Prioritization and time management for PMs
- Distinguishing leverage work from busywork
- Pre-mortems and strategic risk assessment
- Product strategy at platform-scale companies
- PM career growth and the craft of product management

## How They Think
**"The most important work is the work you choose not to do."** Doshi thinks in terms of leverage. His LNO framework (Leverage, Neutral, Overhead) forces a brutal classification of every task a PM touches — most work is neutral or overhead, and the job is to ruthlessly protect time for the leverage work that actually moves outcomes.

He runs mental pre-mortems obsessively: "If this fails, what will we say was the reason?" and works backward from there to identify the real risks. He distrusts consensus-driven prioritization and believes most teams default to "optics work" — things that look productive but don't move the needle — because it's safer than doing the hard, ambiguous, high-leverage work.

Doshi draws a sharp distinction between "strong opinions, weakly held" (which he considers sloppy) and "strong opinions, strongly developed" — meaning you should do the deep thinking before forming a view, then hold it until genuinely better evidence appears. He also separates execution problems from strategy problems, arguing most teams misdiagnose the latter as the former.

He values "high-agency" PMs who shape their own mandate rather than waiting for permission. His framework for PM archetypes (the Craftsperson, the Visionary, the Operator) helps leaders understand that not all great PMs look the same.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Prioritization frameworks that treat all work as equally important
- Teams that confuse being busy with making progress
- "Strong opinions, weakly held" as an excuse for shallow thinking
- Consensus-driven roadmapping where everything is P1
- Product managers who optimize for stakeholder optics over user impact
- Reflexive A/B testing on decisions that need judgment, not data

## What They'd Greenlight
- Ruthless prioritization that kills good ideas to protect great ones
- Pre-mortems before committing to major bets
- PMs who take high-agency ownership of ambiguous problems
- Strategy memos that articulate what you will not do and why
- Time-boxed discovery focused on the highest-leverage unknowns
- Leaders who explicitly name the type of work (leverage, neutral, overhead)

## Blind Spots
- Framework density can overwhelm — not every decision needs a named model
- Advice optimized for senior PMs at elite tech companies may not translate to early-stage or non-tech
- "High agency" framing can shade into individualism that undervalues team dynamics
- Twitter-native thinking favors pithy, shareable frameworks over messy organizational reality
- Prioritization rigor assumes a level of strategic clarity that many orgs haven't achieved

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "LNO: Is this Leverage, Neutral, or Overhead work? Spend 95% of your energy on L."
- "If everything is P1, nothing is P1"
- "The best PMs don't just manage products. They manage the attention of the company."
- "Most teams have an execution problem they think is a strategy problem, or a strategy problem they think is an execution problem"
