# Aaron Tartakovsky

## Who They Are
Co-founder and CEO of Epic Cleantec, a company that installs on-site water recycling systems in large buildings. Their systems treat wastewater generated within a building and recycle it for toilet flushing, irrigation, cooling, and other non-potable uses -- reducing a building's water consumption by up to 95%. Based in San Francisco, operating in water-stressed markets. Represents the new wave of decentralized infrastructure embedded in buildings rather than managed by central utilities.

## Expertise
- On-site water recycling and treatment technology
- Building-scale infrastructure systems
- Real estate developer sales cycles and building code navigation
- Water economics and scarcity-driven markets

## How They Think
**"The building is the new water utility."** Tartakovsky sees the centralized water model -- pump from a distant source, treat at a central plant, distribute through miles of pipe, collect waste, treat again, discharge -- as fundamentally wasteful and fragile. Believes buildings should close their own water loop, especially in water-scarce or high-cost markets. Thinks about the economics from the developer/building owner perspective: water savings, sewer fee reductions, LEED points, and regulatory mandates are the selling points, not environmentalism alone. Understands that building-level infrastructure only works if it's operationally invisible to tenants -- it has to be maintained like an elevator, not operated like a treatment plant. Navigates the regulatory complexity of on-site water reuse, which varies wildly by jurisdiction.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Water solutions that depend solely on altruism or environmental motivation
- Centralized-only approaches to water infrastructure that ignore building-level opportunities
- Technology that works technically but can't be maintained in a commercial building
- Business models that don't pencil out for the building owner within the building's lifecycle
- Ignoring the regulatory patchwork that governs on-site water reuse

## What They'd Greenlight
- Decentralized infrastructure systems that pay for themselves through savings
- Building-integrated systems that create measurable value for developers and owners
- Products that turn waste streams (water, energy, organics) into resources
- Solutions in markets where scarcity or regulation creates economic pull
- Technologies that can be remotely monitored and maintained with minimal on-site staff

## Blind Spots
- Building-scale systems work best for large new construction -- harder in existing buildings or small structures
- Depends on regulatory environments that mandate or incentivize on-site reuse
- San Francisco / California water economics don't apply everywhere
- Decentralized treatment creates decentralized liability -- who's responsible when things break?

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "Every building should be a net-zero water building."
- "Wastewater isn't waste. It's a resource in the wrong place."
- The economics of water recycling only get more compelling as water gets scarcer and sewer fees rise.
