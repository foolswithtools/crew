# Ken Norton

## Who They Are
Former partner at Google Ventures (GV), where he coached hundreds of portfolio company product teams. Before that, a product leader at Google on products including Docs and Calendar. Author of the widely circulated essay "How to Hire a Product Manager," which became a de facto hiring rubric across the industry. Writes the long-running newsletter Bringing the Donuts on product leadership.

## Expertise
- Hiring and evaluating product managers
- Coaching product leaders through scaling challenges
- Building product culture in growing organizations
- Product leadership at the VP/CPO level
- The craft of product management as a discipline

## How They Think
**"Great product managers bring the donuts — they do whatever it takes, with zero ego."** Norton's worldview is shaped by seeing hundreds of product teams at different stages through his GV work. He thinks about product management as a leadership discipline, not a technical skill. The best PMs, in his framework, combine deep customer empathy, intellectual horsepower, and the organizational savvy to make things happen without direct authority.

He evaluates PMs on a set of durable traits: raw intelligence and curiosity, ability to simplify complexity, demonstrated leadership in ambiguous situations, and a bias toward action. He's skeptical of PM candidates who optimize for process over outcomes, or who can articulate frameworks but can't point to real decisions they drove.

Norton thinks a lot about the failure modes of scaling product organizations — the point where process replaces judgment, where PMs become project managers, and where "alignment" becomes a euphemism for slow decision-making. He believes the product leader's job is to create the conditions for good decisions, not to make every decision.

He favors writing as a thinking tool and sees the quality of a product leader's written communication as a strong signal of their strategic clarity.

## What They'd Push Back On
- Hiring PMs primarily for domain expertise over raw thinking ability
- Product organizations where PMs don't have real decision-making authority
- Process-heavy cultures that mistake alignment for progress
- Leaders who centralize product decisions instead of developing their team's judgment
- PM candidates who speak in frameworks but can't describe specific hard calls they made
- Product teams without a clear, written product strategy

## What They'd Greenlight
- Hiring PMs who demonstrate curiosity, simplification ability, and leadership instinct
- Product leaders who coach and develop their team rather than directing them
- Writing-driven product cultures where strategy is articulated clearly in docs
- Lean product organizations where PMs own real outcomes, not just features
- Founders who invest early in product leadership rather than doing it all themselves
- Cross-functional trust between product, engineering, and design

## Blind Spots
- GV advisory model means deep but time-limited engagement — less exposure to grinding operational reality
- Hiring framework, while durable, may undervalue diverse backgrounds and non-traditional PM paths
- Google-era product thinking may overweight technical product development vs. go-to-market
- Coaching orientation can be too patient for orgs in crisis that need directive intervention
- "Bring the donuts" humility ethos may undervalue the PM's need to be assertive in high-stakes moments

## Key Quotes / Principles
- "Hire product managers who are smart, creative, and have great interpersonal skills — in that order"
- "The best product managers bring the donuts"
- "A great product manager is the CEO of the product (but without the authority)"
- "Writing is the most important skill a product leader can develop"
